# Web Usability

- One of the quality metrics for a website
- Measures the degree of "user comfort" when using the site
- A well-usable website:
    - Visitors can easily find their way around
    - They understand it
    - It does not force users to think — it is *intuitive*

> Approximately **40% of visitors** will not return to a site if their initial experience was negative. About **50% of sales are lost** if visitors cannot find what they are looking for fast enough.
> <span>— Jakob Nielsen and Forrester Research (Failure of Corporate Websites, useit.com, 1998)</span>

---

# Importance of Usability

- For commercial websites, usability means **revenue**
    - Poor usability can deter a customer from completing an order
- Properly conducted **usability tests** can reveal obstacles when searching for specific information

> Studies of user behaviour on the web found a low tolerance for complex designs and slow sites. People **do not want to wait, and do not want to learn** how to use a website.
> <span>— Jakob Nielsen</span>

---

# Usability Factors

- **Learnability**
    - How quickly can a new user complete basic tasks?
- **Efficiency**
    - How efficiently can an experienced user complete assigned tasks?
- **Memorability**
    - Is it easy to remember how to complete a given task?
- **Error tolerance**
    - How easy is it to make a mistake, and how easily can it be corrected?
- **Subjective satisfaction**
    - Do users *enjoy* using your site?

---

# Usability vs. Accessibility

- Two often-confused areas
- **Accessibility** — is the content of the site *technically available* to the user?
- **Usability** — is the user able to *make use of* the content they can access?

<div class="textbox">

**Accessibility is a necessary but not sufficient condition for usability.**

</div>

# Designing a Usable Website

1. Planning
    - What do we want to achieve?
2. User data collection
    - Who will use the site, and what will they expect from it?
3. Prototyping
    - A preliminary version of the site for testing
4. Content collection, writing and revision
    - Selection, organisation and form of content presentation
5. Usability testing
    - Preferably with real users
6. Site promotion and maintenance

---

# Phase 1: Planning

- Planning helps precisely define the project goals
- First, clarify:
    1. Why we are building the site
    2. Who should use it
    3. When and why they should use it

---

# Why Are We Building the Site?

- Design **measurable and controllable goals**
- Answer the following questions:
    - How will we know (quantitatively) that the site is successful?
    - What will the consequences be if the site is not successful?
- Avoid overly generic goals
    - E.g. *"to inform visitors about our products"*
- Better to tie goals to business needs:
    - E.g. *"increase mountain bike sales by 30%"*
    - *"Reduce the load on telephone user support by 50%"*

---

# Who Should Use the Site?

- List the target groups
- Example basic breakdown for an e-commerce site:
    - Users gathering information about products (specs, price, ...) — *"browsers"*
    - Users intending to purchase a specific product — *"buyers"*
- Depends on the site's focus, e.g. a government institution's site:
    - Citizen, business owner, journalist, ...
- Develop a profile for each group:
    - E.g. employed, looking for details, domain-knowledgeable, little web experience
    - These are estimates — they will be verified during the testing phase
- These profiles must be respected in the site's design

---

# When and Why Users Come

- Users arrive with their own goal in mind
- Create typical scenarios:
    - E.g. *Karl's TV has broken down and he needs to buy a new one with similar specs to his old device as quickly as possible.*
    - *Josef has won the lottery and wants to buy a top-of-the-range TV, but he does not know exactly what specs to look for.*
- Further scenarios can be obtained directly from users in later steps

---

# Phase 2: User Data Collection

- The best way to learn about users is to:
    - **Ask them**
    - **Work with them**
- We want to gather information about:
    - What information they need
    - How they think about and organise information
    - What they expect from the site
    - Their level of domain knowledge
    - Their level of web experience
- We can collect many real usage scenarios

---

# User Data Collection Techniques

- **Preliminary usability test**
    - Test on the current (old) site, if available, or on a competitor's site
    - Small group of test users (5–12)
    - Observe user behaviour while completing tasks
    - Can also be done remotely (log analysis)
- **Contextual interview**
    - Observe the user in their usual working environment
    - Typically no special tasks are assigned
    - Note environmental characteristics:
        - Technical conditions
        - Workplace atmosphere
        - Can the user ask someone for help?

---

# User Data Collection Techniques (II)

- **Online surveys**
    - Research using web questionnaires
    - Users fill in information about themselves
    - Important parameters:
        - What information we want to obtain
        - Where we will find respondents
        - The questionnaire should be short (up to 10 questions)
        - Should not be time-consuming to complete
        - Combination of questions about the user (demographics) and their preferences

---

# User Data Collection Techniques (III)

- **Individual interviews**
    - Smaller number of respondents (around 15)
    - We do not observe behaviour — we gather expectations and preferences
    - Usually a conversation based on a predetermined outline
    - The interview should be conducted by an experienced professional
- **Group discussion (focus group)**
    - Group of 8–12 respondents
    - We gather:
        - User attitudes and requirements
        - Reactions and ideas about prototypes
    - Best led by an experienced moderator

---

# User Data Collection Techniques (IV)

- **Card sorting**
    - The user participates in the data organisation process
    - The user receives a set of cards with the site's content topics
    - They place the first card on the table
    - For each subsequent card, they decide whether it belongs in the same category or deserves a new one
    - They can rearrange cards at any time
    - The user should *"think aloud"*

---

# Phase 3: Prototyping

- A prototype is a *"mock-up"* of the future site
- Typically does not contain all content
- Contains a sample home page and several pages of different types
- The goal is to have a demonstration of information architecture, navigation and design for user testing
- Allows the design to be verified before investing time and resources into building the full site
- Should be easy to adapt based on findings

---

# Phase 4: Content Creation

- **Content selection**
    - Is each piece of information relevant to the site's topic?
    - Does the user really need this information?
- **Content organisation**
    - Content must be *"easy to use"*
    - Written in clear language
    - Presented in an accessible form
    - Logically organised

---

# Logical Content Organisation

- Break content into manageable chunks
    - Content must not put readers off with its length and complexity
    - Short paragraphs and sentences
    - Lists, tables, images
    - Examples
- Frequent use of headings
    - Simplify orientation on the page
    - Make the page look more interesting
    - Every short section and table should have a heading
- Links to important headings at the top (anchors)
    - For longer documents
    - Help with navigation

---

# Logical Content Organisation (II)

- Sequential information must be presented in the correct order
    - E.g. understanding one section requires knowledge of another
- If order does not matter, prioritise information that is more important to the user

---

# Content Presentation

- Use of white space
    - Information should cluster naturally
    - Too much white space feels empty
    - Too little white space — information merges and does not form *"clusters"*
- Effective expression
    - Every sentence must have meaning — no *"filler words"*
    - Repeated revisions with time gaps

---

# Content Presentation (II)

- Language used
    - The site should speak the user's language
    - Avoid specialist *"jargon"*
- Lists clarify content
    - Bullet points are more comprehensible than long sentences
    - A numbered list is suitable for step-by-step instructions
- Tables
    - More readable than a verbal description
    - E.g. price for individuals, price for companies, ...

---

# Content Presentation (III)

- Examples
    - Users often prefer an example to reading through lengthy theory
- Established presentation forms
    - Many pieces of information are conventionally presented in a specific way
    - E.g. addresses, prices, ...
- Icons
    - Liven up the page
    - Emphasise the meaning of words (e.g. phone, ...)
- Frequent use of images (where appropriate)

---

# Users on the Web

- A typical user is in a hurry
    - Looking for specific information
    - Has plenty of other things to do
    - Many pages to browse through
- Web users do not *read*
    - They only *"scan"* the content of a document
    - If a document does not look relevant, they look for another
- Reading on a screen can be harder
    - Better to present key information with a link to the full document
    - Provide print-friendly versions of important documents

---

# Phase 5: Usability Testing

- Testing goals:
    - Can users complete the given task?
    - How quickly?
    - Are they satisfied with that speed?
    - By which paths do they reach the result?
    - Do they find those paths efficient?
    - What are the most frequent problem areas?
    - Do they try approaches the site does not support?

---

# Phase 6: Promotion and Maintenance

- The site must be findable
    - Placing links on other sites
    - Attractive content ensures more frequent linking
    - Search engine optimisation (SEO)
    - Press releases
    - Regular updates = visitors return more often
- Site maintenance
    - Content updates
    - Link checking
- Future redesign
    - Requirements, technology or users may change
